Physics-aware abstention · proof-of-concept audit

Can a model tell when the physics doesn't add up?

We train small networks to invert two physiological laws — pulse-oximetry (Beer–Lambert) and cuffless blood-pressure (Moens–Korteweg) — then ask a frozen linear probe on their internal activations to flag inputs where the governing equation is non-identifiable: a required signal is missing or corrupted, so no correct answer exists. Gating on that probe should let the model restrain itself when it cannot know.

Honest
verdict

The abstention machinery works: probe-ranked gating beats random, and ablating the probe direction provably degrades the readout — robustly, both laws, all seeds. But detection leans heavily on trivial missingness (a zeroed channel); on harder held-out corruptions where every channel is present but the physics is subtly wrong, Beer–Lambert detection collapses to chance. Neither law clears the full pre-registered five-criterion bar. This is a proof-of-concept for the easy case and a precise map of what the hard case needs — not a solved problem.

01

The question, precisely

Most "does the model know what it doesn't know" work labels "unknown" by a proxy. Here the label is a mathematical fact.

A governing equation y = f(x) is identifiable only when every variable its inverse requires is present in the input. Remove one — zero a wavelength channel, withhold a calibration constant — and the inverse problem becomes underdetermined: infinitely many answers fit what's observed. Because we build the simulator, we know this label by construction, exactly. That gives verifiable ground-truth epistemic labels — the piece almost every uncertainty-probing paper lacks — and lets us ask whether the network's own activations encode that boundary.

02

Two governing laws, two ways to be unanswerable

Different physics, different failure modes — a missing sensor vs. a missing constant.

Beer–Lambert

Pulse oximetry · SpO₂
Iλ = I₀·exp(−μλ·c·L(t))
R = (ACred/DCred) / (ACir/DCir) → SpO₂

Identifiable when both wavelengths are present: the ratio-of-ratios R needs red and IR.

✕ unanswerable missing channel · wavelength mismatch · severe motion

Moens–Korteweg

Cuffless blood pressure · BP
PWV = √(E·h / (ρ·D)) · PTT = L / PWV
E(P) = E₀·exp(α·P/100) → BP needs calibration

Identifiable when transit path and subject stiffness calibration are known: uncalibrated PTT does not fix BP.

✕ unanswerable missing calibration · missing path length · missing distal wave

The honest limitation

On the all-scenarios number both laws look great (0.71, 1.00). But that is dominated by the trivial case — a zeroed channel is easy to spot. On held-out non-missing corruptions, Beer–Lambert detection is at or below chance (0.47 / 0.52 / 0.30). Moens–Korteweg's perfect held-out score is itself inflated, because its held-out set still contains one zeroed-channel scenario. So "the model knows it can't answer" holds strongly for missing information and remains unproven for subtly-wrong-but-complete information — the case that actually matters clinically.

Is the "I can't answer" signal universal across laws?

Train the answerability signature on one law, test zero-shot on the other. A compact 3-feature signature (residual, activation-norm, probe confidence) — raw activations can't transfer (different shapes). Mean of 3 seeds.

Within-domain (ceiling) Cross-law transfer (zero-shot) Chance = 0.5

Sharp, reproducible asymmetry: Moens–Korteweg → Beer–Lambert transfers moderately (0.75), but Beer–Lambert → Moens–Korteweg is below chance (0.31). This is exactly what the theory predicts — MK's identifiability gap is large and clean (missing calibration makes BP genuinely undecidable), so its fitted direction carries transferable signal; Beer's gap is smaller and noisier, so its direction does not. The signal is a domain-general mechanism, not a domain-general direction.

10

Why it works when it works: the identifiability gap

A one-line theory that makes the results predictable rather than lucky.

A Bayes-consistent readout cannot drive its loss below the irreducible variance of y given what it observes. On unanswerable inputs that variance is strictly higher — call the ratio the identifiability gap γ > 1. So the model's own pointwise loss (and anything linearly correlated with it in the activations) must separate answerable from unanswerable, with a margin that grows with γ. The prediction: detection AUROC → 1 as γ → ∞, → 0.5 as γ → 1. That is precisely the pattern observed — MK's large clean γ gives near-perfect, transferable detection; Beer's small γ gives weak, non-transferable detection. No epistemic label is ever needed; the signal is a structural by-product of training on the forward task. Full derivation in analysis/THEORY.md.

11

What this is, and what it would take to be more

  • Is: an honest proof-of-concept that frozen activations encode identifiability, with a mechanism (γ), pre-registered criteria, and reported negative results. Workshop-grade as-is.
  • Missingness shortcut: the #1 next measurement is isolating held-out detection on non-missing corruptions only — the case that separates real epistemic sensitivity from "spot the zero."
  • The Toy-Models move: replace the messy physiological laws with a minimal y = Ax instrument where removing rows of A tunes γ exactly, and sweep γ looking for a phase transition in decodability — that is the phenomenon study with real main-track potential.
  • Then: more laws (Fick, Windkessel), real data (BIDMC / MIMIC), and 20–30 seeds with the training instability fixed.
  • Don't overclaim: this is linear probing + one causal ablation — representation-level, not circuit-level "mechanistic."
